There are 374,256 observations in the dataset. Each observation is an ACT test taken by a student in Southern Minnesota. The unique feature here is the time it was taken. This means that a student could have taken the ACT twice and their PersonID would show up as two observations.

Out of the scores provided, Iâ€™m just going to use the composite score. Since an individual may have taken the test multiple times I will only use the highest composite score for each individual. This is how colleges do it as well.


act.highest.comp <- act.original %>%
  select(PersonID, ACTCompositeScore) %>%
  mutate(PersonID = as.integer(PersonID)) %>%
  group_by(PersonID) %>%
  filter(ACTCompositeScore == max(ACTCompositeScore)) %>%
  ungroup() %>%
  distinct(PersonID, .keep_all = TRUE)

Summary of ACT data

First, lets see what percentage of observations took the ACT and what the average composite score was.

The table below shows that just over two-thirds of graduates from Southern Minnesota have taken the ACT at some point in the dataset.



Next lets see if the ACT taken rate differs across RUCA, regions and counties.

The cross-tabs for the percentage of students who have taken the ACT by RUCA category has a p-value of less than .01, indicating that RUCA category is related. The trend shows that the more rural a school district, the lower the percentage of graduates that have taken the ACT - town/rural mix had 66% of their graduates take the ACT whereas entirely urban districts had 73%.
