Introduction

The previous analysis focused on how demographics, high school characteristics, accomplishments, and postsecondary experiences predict meaningful employment in the region versus disconnection or statewide mobility. The findings highlighted postsecondary experiences as the most powerful predictors of long-term outcomes, with clear dual pipelines: CTE and sub-baccalaureate pathways anchoring students regionally, and academic rigor with higher credentials leading to statewide mobility.

Given the centrality of postsecondary variables in shaping workforce attachment and mobility, the next phase of analysis shifts focus. Specifically, we examine how demographic background, high school characteristics, and high school accomplishments influence key postsecondary outcomes such as:

  • Whether students attend a postsecondary institution.
  • Whether they graduate from postsecondary.
  • The sector and location of their postsecondary institution.
  • The highest credential level attained.

By analyzing these linkages, we can identify which student attributes and high school experiences most strongly predict postsecondary pathways, and in turn, how these pathways shape regional workforce retention. This step deepens the understanding of how early life circumstances and schooling experiences channel students into postsecondary trajectories that later drive employment outcomes.


Methodology

To evaluate the relationships between student attributes and postsecondary experiences, the analysis uses a combination of cross-tabulations, Cramer’s V effect size, and ANOVA tests, following the same approach applied in the prior employment-focused analysis.

  1. Cross-tabulations were constructed between demographic variables (e.g., gender, race/ethnicity, economic status, special education), high school characteristics (e.g., rural/metro classification, county wage/unemployment context), and accomplishments (e.g., CTE engagement, ACT/AP participation, MCA performance) against postsecondary outcomes. These tables provide a descriptive view of group-level differences.

  2. Cramer’s V was calculated for categorical relationships to assess the strength of association between predictor variables and postsecondary outcomes. Effect sizes were interpreted on a conventional scale (weak, moderate, strong) to prioritize meaningful predictors.

  3. ANOVA tests were used where continuous predictors (e.g., test scores, number of CTE courses taken) were compared across postsecondary outcome categories. This allowed for assessing mean differences and the statistical significance of those differences.

Results will again be summarized across three tiers of predictive value: strong predictors (clear, consistent associations), moderate predictors (some association, less consistent), and weak or inconsistent predictors (low effect sizes, candidates for exclusion). This structure provides both clarity and comparability with the employment outcome analysis, while highlighting the mechanisms by which students are sorted into postsecondary pathways that ultimately drive workforce outcomes.

Total Summary: Predictors of Postsecondary Attendance (attended.ps)

Across demographics, high school characteristics, and accomplishments, clear patterns emerge in who is most likely to pursue postsecondary education. Academic achievement and early exposure to postsecondary opportunities are the strongest predictors, while economic disadvantage and special education needs are the most powerful barriers. High school context measures show weaker associations, suggesting that student-level factors matter more than community background.

1. Strong Predictors of Postsecondary Attendance

These variables show strong or moderate associations and consistently differentiate postsecondary attendance outcomes.

Variable Type Key Insights
MCA Reading, Math, Science Accomplishment (continuous) Higher MCA scores strongly predict postsecondary attendance, with Reading the single strongest indicator.
ACTCompositeScore Accomplishment (continuous) Higher ACT scores significantly increase likelihood of attending PS.
pseo.participant Accomplishment (categorical) Participation in PSEO strongly boosts PS attendance, highlighting the value of early postsecondary exposure.
SpecialEdStatus Demographic Students in special education are far less likely to attend PS, making this one of the most powerful barriers.
Economic Status Demographic Low-income students are much less likely to attend PS, showing persistent socioeconomic disparities.

2. Moderate Predictors — Secondary but Meaningful

These variables show moderate or weaker effects, useful for understanding disparities but less decisive than the strongest predictors.

Variable Type Key Insights
RaceEthnicity Demographic White and Asian/PI students most likely to attend PS; Black, Hispanic, and American Indian students less likely.
Gender Demographic Females somewhat more likely to attend PS than males, but the gap is modest.
total.cte.courses.taken Accomplishment (continuous) Taking more CTE courses correlates with higher PS attendance, but effect is modest compared to academics.

3. Weak or Inconsistent Predictors — Limited Value

These variables show weak associations and are less useful in predicting postsecondary attendance independently.

Variable Type Notes
cte.achievement Accomplishment (categorical) Little difference across CTE participants, concentrators, or non-participants in terms of PS attendance.
Dem_Desc HS Characteristic Urban, rural, and mixed settings show almost no independent effect on PS attendance.
avg.unemp.rate HS Characteristic (continuous) Significant differences observed, but effect size small; stronger economies modestly raise PS attendance.
avg.wages.pct.state HS Characteristic (continuous) Higher relative local wages linked to higher PS attendance, though weaker than unemployment rate differences.

Conclusion

The most consistent predictors of postsecondary attendance are academic achievement (MCA and ACT scores), PSEO participation, and demographic disadvantages (special education and low-income status). Race, gender, and CTE course-taking add nuance but are secondary factors. High school context measures such as community type, local unemployment, and local wages matter less than individual-level academic and demographic characteristics.

In short: academic readiness and early exposure to college drive postsecondary entry, while economic disadvantage and special education needs are the greatest barriers.

Interpretation of Results

  • SpecialEdStatus: Students in special education are substantially less likely to attend postsecondary. The split is stark: most non-SPED students go on, while nearly two-thirds of SPED students do not. This makes it the most powerful demographic predictor.
  • Economic Status: Low-income students are much less likely to attend postsecondary compared to their non-low-income peers. The difference is meaningful but smaller than SPED.
  • RaceEthnicity: Attendance patterns vary by group. White and Asian/PI students show the highest participation, while American Indian, Hispanic, and Black students are less likely to attend. Disparities are clear but not as large as the income and SPED gaps.
  • Gender: Females are somewhat more likely to attend postsecondary than males, but the effect is weaker compared to other demographics.

Conclusion

Among demographic factors, special education status and economic status are the strongest predictors of postsecondary attendance, both showing moderate effect sizes. Race/ethnicity matters, with persistent gaps across groups, but its predictive strength is weaker. Gender plays only a minor role, with females more likely to attend but not dramatically so.

In short: SPED and income disadvantage most strongly limit postsecondary access, while racial/ethnic disparities and gender patterns play secondary roles.

  1. avg.unemp.rate (most statistically significant mean difference)
  2. avg.wages.pct.state (significant but smaller than unemployment)

Interpretation of Results

  • Dem_Desc: Urban, rural, and mixed community contexts show little independent relationship to postsecondary attendance once other factors are considered. Students from all community types attend at broadly similar rates.
  • avg.unemp.rate: Students in counties with lower unemployment rates are significantly more likely to attend postsecondary. This suggests stronger local labor markets reduce immediate employment pressure and encourage PS attendance.
  • avg.wages.pct.state: Higher local wages (relative to state average) are also linked to higher PS attendance, though the effect is weaker than unemployment. Better local economic conditions appear to create more postsecondary participation.

Conclusion

High school context measures show weak-to-modest associations with postsecondary attendance. Local economic indicators (unemployment and wages) matter more than rural/urban community type, suggesting economic opportunity environments influence whether students pursue college. However, compared to demographic factors like special education and income status, these contextual factors are secondary predictors.

Total Summary: Predictors of Postsecondary Graduation by Location (ps.grad.location)

Graduates sort into three categories: Southern MN completions, Minnesota outside the region, and out-of-state completions. The clearest pattern is that demographic disadvantage and CTE engagement anchor students regionally, while academic rigor and PSEO participation propel students toward mobility. This sorting mechanism directly ties to workforce outcomes: regional completions are most strongly linked to meaningful employment in Southern MN, while out-of-region completions align with statewide mobility.

Graduation location reflects a dual system:

  • CTE engagement, low-income status, SPED identification, and disadvantaged local economies anchor students into Southern MN completions, tying them to the regional workforce.

In short: regional completions are the strongest predictor of regional workforce retention, and these are most common among students facing economic or educational disadvantage and those engaged in CTE. Grand Synthesis: Predictors of Postsecondary Pathways and Their Relationship to Regional Employment

Across all four postsecondary outcomes — attended.ps, ps.grad, ps.grad.InstitutionSector, ps.grad.location, and highest.cred.level — consistent patterns emerge about which factors shape educational attainment and how these pathways tie into regional workforce retention versus mobility and disconnection.

The evidence is clear: postsecondary experiences are the strongest predictors of long-term employment outcomes, but they are deeply influenced by demographic background, high school context, and student accomplishments. This creates a dual-pipeline system: one that anchors students to Southern MN through CTE and sub-baccalaureate credentials, and another that channels academically strong students into statewide or national mobility through 4-year and higher pathways.

1. Attending Postsecondary Education

  • Key Predictors: Economic status (low-income less likely to attend), race/ethnicity (persistent gaps), and special education status (lower attendance).
  • Takeaway: Disadvantaged students are disproportionately less likely to enroll in PS at all, putting them at high risk of long-term disconnection.

2. Postsecondary Graduation (ps.grad)

  • Key Predictors: Economic status and SPED remain barriers to completion; racial gaps persist; academic readiness improves odds of graduating.
  • Takeaway: Simply completing PS dramatically reduces disconnection and boosts employment. Non-completers remain the most vulnerable group in the workforce pipeline.

3. Institution Sector of Graduation (ps.grad.InstitutionSector)

  • Key Predictors:
    • Academic rigor and PSEO → 4-year completions.
    • CTE engagement, SPED, and low-income → 2-year completions.
  • Takeaway: Institution type sorts students into applied versus academic pathways. Since 2-year completions are more strongly tied to regional employment, CTE and disadvantage groups inadvertently form the backbone of Southern MN’s workforce, while academically strong students pursue more mobile 4-year pathways.

4. Location of Graduation (ps.grad.location)

  • Key Predictors:
    • Disadvantaged students (low-income, SPED, marginalized race/ethnicity) → more likely to graduate in Southern MN.
    • High scorers and PSEO participants → more likely to graduate outside the region (other MN or out-of-state).
    • Local economic conditions (lower wages, higher unemployment) → stronger Southern MN completions.
  • Takeaway: Regional completions are most common among disadvantaged groups and CTE students, reinforcing their role in regional workforce stability.

5. Highest Credential Earned (highest.cred.level)

  • Key Predictors:
    • Academic rigor (ACT, MCA) and PSEO → bachelor’s or higher.
    • CTE engagement, low-income, SPED, disadvantaged counties → associate or sub-associate.
  • Takeaway: Credential level is the clearest stratifier of opportunity. Bachelor’s+ aligns with mobility, while associate/sub-associate aligns with regional employment.

Overall Conclusion

The postsecondary pipeline is shaped by two reinforcing forces:

  1. Mobility Pipeline:
    • Driven by academic rigor and PSEO.
    • Leads to 4-year+ degrees, completions outside the region, and statewide mobility.
    • Most common among advantaged, higher-scoring students.
  2. Regional Pipeline:
    • Driven by CTE engagement, 2-year institutions, economic disadvantage, SPED, and lower-wage/unemployment counties.
    • Leads to associate/sub-associate degrees and Southern MN completions, strongly tied to meaningful regional employment.
    • Most common among disadvantaged students who remain anchored locally.

In short: Southern MN’s workforce stability relies heavily on the regional pipeline of 2-year and CTE-driven pathways, which disproportionately serve low-income and SPED students. Meanwhile, academically strong and advantaged students pursue 4-year and higher credentials, fueling statewide and out-of-state mobility.

Policy Implication: To strengthen the regional workforce, interventions should both (1) support disadvantaged students in completing and leveraging their 2-year pathways and (2) create incentives for academically strong students to remain engaged in the region after completing higher degrees.
